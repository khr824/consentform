Qualitative Research Informed Consent

A guide to structuring and writing ethical consent forms for interviews, focus groups, and observations.

The Structure of a Consent Form

A well-structured consent form guides the participant logically through the study details, ensuring they understand what they are agreeing to before they sign. Keep the language accessible (usually at an 8th-grade reading level).

1. Heading and Introduction

  • Study Title: Clear and easy to understand (avoid excessive academic jargon).
  • Researcher Information: Name, institutional affiliation, and role (e.g., Lead Researcher, PhD Candidate).
  • Invitation: A brief sentence inviting them to participate in the research.

2. The "Why" and "What"

  • Purpose of the Study: A brief explanation of what the research aims to discover.
  • Study Procedures: What the participant will actually do (e.g., "You will participate in a 60-minute audio-recorded interview").

3. Risks, Benefits, and Compensation

  • Potential Risks: In qualitative research, this often includes emotional discomfort, fatigue, or a breach of confidentiality.
  • Potential Benefits: Direct benefits to the participant (often none) and broader benefits to society or the field of study.
  • Compensation: Details about any stipends, gift cards, or course credit offered, and the conditions for receiving them.

4. Data Management and Rights

  • Confidentiality & Privacy: How the data will be protected (e.g., using pseudonyms, secure servers).
  • Voluntary Nature of the Study: Explicitly stating that participation is 100% voluntary and they can withdraw at any time without penalty.

5. Conclusion and Signatures

  • Contact Information: Who to contact with questions about the study or their rights as a participant (usually includes the researcher and the Institutional Review Board/Ethics Committee).
  • Statement of Consent: The actual agreement text.
  • Signature Lines: Spaces for the participant's name, signature, and date, plus optional checkboxes for specific permissions (like recording).

What to Include in a Qualitative Consent Form

Qualitative research (like interviews and focus groups) gathers rich, personal data. Your consent form must address specific elements unique to this methodology.

Key Rule: Never promise "anonymity" in qualitative face-to-face interviews or focus groups, as the researcher knows who the participant is, and focus group members know each other. Promise confidentiality instead.

Essential Inclusions for Qualitative Forms:

  • Audio/Video Recording Consent: Qualitative data relies heavily on recordings. You must explicitly ask for permission to record. (e.g., "Do you consent to having this interview audio-recorded?")
  • Use of Direct Quotes: Explain that you may use their exact words in your final report, but clarify how they will be attributed. (e.g., "Direct quotes may be used in publications, but they will be attributed to a pseudonym.")
  • Data Retention and Destruction: Be specific about what happens to the recordings and transcripts. (e.g., "Audio recordings will be destroyed immediately after transcription. De-identified transcripts will be kept on a secure server for 5 years.")
  • Right to Skip Questions: Make it clear that in an interview setting, the participant does not have to answer every question.
  • Limits of Confidentiality: State clearly if there are legal limits to confidentiality (e.g., mandatory reporting of abuse or intent to harm).
  • Member Checking (Optional but recommended): Let them know if they will have the opportunity to review their transcript or the findings before publication.
  • Focus Group Clause (If applicable): If conducting a focus group, include a statement asking all participants to respect the privacy of others and keep the conversation confidential, while acknowledging that the researcher cannot guarantee what other participants will do.
